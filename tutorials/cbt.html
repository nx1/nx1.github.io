I think it could be worth looking a littled deeper at some of the points I
alluded to prior here, namely first the ticking clock.

In the Denial of Death (Becker 1973), Becker suggests that humans are split
into two the physical body and trapped inside the symbolic (mind). The mind,
understanding that the physical body that houses it will die, tries to deal with
this mortality by creating a life project (causa sui) that may outlive them both.

The causa sui may come in many forms: a career, a book, amassing wealth, creating
a subculture scene or raising a family, these things allow us to believe we are
participating in something eternal that will outlive us, if the project continues
then we never truly die. (something something you die twice etc :,) ).

From Becker's perspective the ticking clock relating to optimal ages for
children or family is not just a spacio-temporal race but actually a crackling
of the cultural illusion. The anxiety spikes when we see our physical body
winning the race against our symbolic self.






CBT Backstory


Psychoanalysts, Freud et al., used to take the approach that most problems were
caused by disruptions in the unconscious mind, a partially true observation but
not the entire story. Not all problems are fixed by digging up suppressed
childhood trauma that manifests in one's dreams.

In the 1920s-50s, behavioral psychologists like B.F. Skinnerrealized that
scientifically measuring a person's unconscious turned out to be quite tricky,
but measuring their behavior was a lot easier. The new focus shifted to
understanding human conditioning, that is to say: how do rewards and
punishments shape people's actions? E.g., if you have a phobia of something,
repeated exposure to that thing reduces the fear as your nervous system adapts
to the stimulus.

In the 1960s, Aaron Beck observed a common trend amongst depressed patients:
many had a running commentary of negative thoughts about themselves and the
world: "I'm a failure", "Things will never get better". An observation that
seems extraordinarily mundane by today's standards I mean, that's what we all
think when we think depression, right? Well, that's because the word has spread
far after 60 years, or maybe there's just way more depressed people today lol,
who knows.

In any case, it was clear that these thoughts were not facts; they were
irrational and distorted ways of thinking what he called "automatic thoughts".
These thoughts would fall into one of three categories: about oneself, the
world, or the future. He argued that if you could change the way people
thought, you could reduce the emotional distress these people were under. By
bringing attention to these thoughts in the people who thought them, it was
found that patients could think more realistically and rationally about them,
which often helped them feel better and behave more functionally. Basically, by
changing the way you think, you can change the outcome of things. Combined with
the knowledge of his contemporaries, this gave birth to the thing known as
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT).

Basic premise of the theory is that thoughts, feelings, and behavior are all
closely related. A prime example of this is the combination:

Thought:  "If I speak to them they'll think I'm weird."
Feeling:  "Anxiety, Fight/Flight, discomfort."
Behavior: "Don't talk to them, avoid."

The behavior then reinforces the thought; each time the act is repeated, the
mind learns the pattern and therefore learns the feeling and learns the
behavior.

CBT is essentially a way of breaking out of these cycles. This can be done by
abstracting it out and seeing it from the outside—realizing what the pattern is
and working to break it. Repeating the new pattern enough times then acts to
reinforce this new thought, feeling, and behavior. The amount of resistance
that it would take to override existing processes will likely depend on many
factors, such as how deeply ingrained it is. For example, trying to use CBT to
correct a crack addiction may be quite difficult, but not impossible?
